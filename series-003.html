There is a woman you know.

You may not have thought of her in these terms before, but you know her. She is the one who woke before everyone else. Who knew, without being told, that the food needed to be ready before the visitors arrived. Who sat with the sick child through the night and still appeared in the morning, functional, present, ready. Who remembered every birthday, every grudge that needed softening, every relationship in the compound that was fraying and required attention before it broke.

She did not have a job title. She did not have a salary. She did not have a performance review.

She had a compound. She had a family. She had a community that ran, quietly and continuously, on her labour.

In software, there is a concept called a silent contributor — someone whose work makes everything else possible but whose name does not appear in the commit history. They reviewed the architecture before it was built. They caught the errors before they shipped. They held the team together during the months when the project nearly collapsed. But the repository has no record of them. The changelog is silent. The credits list the people who typed the code.

African communities have always had silent contributors. They are not hard to find. They are everywhere. They are, in fact, the reason everything is still standing.

The woman who co-ordinates the age grade meeting, mediates between families, ensures that the youngest members are fed and the oldest are visited. The aunt who takes in the child whose parents can no longer manage. The neighbour who knows which family is struggling and routes resources to them without announcement. The mother who does not sleep until every person in the household is accounted for.

None of this is in any database. None of it is measured. None of it has a line in any national budget.

And yet without it, nothing else functions.

Think about what a Nigerian funeral actually requires.

Not the church service, not the programme of events, not the rented canopies and the hired caterers. Those are visible. Those get planned. Think about the weeks before: the women who arrive and begin cooking at four in the morning. The people who know instinctively how to receive grief, how to sit with a family, what to say and when to say nothing. The co-ordination that happens through networks of relationship so old and dense that no event management company could replicate them.

Think about what a Nigerian market actually requires.

Not the stalls and the goods. The informal credit that flows between traders who have known each other for decades. The woman who will hold your money because you trust her more than you trust a bank. The dispute resolution that happens in the open air, mediated by people with no formal authority and complete social authority.

Think about what a Nigerian school actually requires.

Not the building, not the curriculum, not the exam board. The mother who supplements what the school cannot afford to teach. Who buys the textbooks. Who sits with the homework. Who translates between the formal system and the child who is confused by it.

None of these people are in the GDP figures. None of this labour appears in the development reports. The international agencies fly in, count what they can count, and fly out. The spreadsheets show a gap where the informal economy should be. The women filling that gap are not in the spreadsheet.

There is a word that gets used sometimes in economic literature: externality. A cost or benefit that exists outside the transaction. Something real that the model does not capture.

African women's labour has been treated as an externality for as long as development economics has existed. Present, yes. Acknowledged, occasionally. Counted, never.

This is not an oversight. Oversights get corrected when someone points them out. This has been pointed out, repeatedly, for decades. It persists because counting it would require reckoning with it — and reckoning with it would require paying for it. And paying for it would restructure every conversation about African poverty, African productivity, African development.

The invisibility is load-bearing. The system depends on not seeing her.

But she is there.

She was there before the colonial administrators arrived with their censuses and their categories. She was there when the missionaries built their churches and wrote their records and left her out of them. She was there when the independence leaders drafted their constitutions. She is there now, while the development goals are written in glass buildings by people who will never sit with her in her compound.

She is the infrastructure. She is the institution. She is, in the most precise sense of the word, the system.

The repo has no record of her.

The repo would not exist without her.

There is a financial system that has operated across Africa for centuries without a central bank, a regulatory framework, or a single line of code.

You probably know it by one of its names. Esusu in Yoruba communities. Ajo. Adashe in the North. Susu in Ghana. Tontine across francophone Africa. The names change with the geography. The logic is identical: a group of people pool a fixed contribution regularly, and each member receives the total sum in rotation, until everyone has had their turn.

No interest. No collateral. No application form. No credit score.

Just trust, and the social cost of breaking it.

This system has capitalised small businesses, funded school fees, financed weddings and burials and home renovations, across generations, in communities where formal banks either did not exist or did not consider the people there worth serving. It has moved value quietly and efficiently through the informal economy at a scale that no microfinance institution has matched — because it was not designed by an institution. It was designed by people who understood their community and built something that fit.

It is not in any budget. It does not appear in financial inclusion reports as a success story, because no one deployed it. It was already there.

Africa runs on infrastructure that nobody planned and nobody funds.

The age grade system that organises community labour — clearing roads, maintaining public spaces, enforcing social norms, mobilising for collective projects — without a payroll or a project manager. The kinship networks that function as a social security system: when a family member loses a job, falls ill, or dies, the network activates. People appear. Resources move. The gap gets filled, not because there is a policy, but because there is a relationship.

The market women's associations that regulate trade, mediate disputes, set standards, and provide credit to members. The town unions that fund boreholes and scholarships and road repairs in communities where the government has not arrived and may never arrive. The religious communities that run feeding programmes, skills training, and counselling without a grant or a nonprofit registration.

These are not gaps in the formal system waiting to be filled. They are not primitive precursors to something more sophisticated. They are sophisticated. They are, in many cases, more responsive, more accountable, and more efficient than the formal systems that development agencies spend billions trying to build alongside them.

They are load-bearing walls. And the people who build them — who maintain them, who give their time and knowledge and relational capital to keep them standing — are doing this for free.

The development industry has a particular relationship with African informal systems.

It studies them. It writes papers about them. It occasionally tries to formalise them — to take the esusu and turn it into a regulated savings product, to take the community health worker and absorb her into a government payroll, to take the town union and register it as an NGO with reporting requirements and donor compliance frameworks.

Sometimes this improves things. Often it breaks them. The thing that made the informal system work was precisely its informality: the social accountability, the flexibility, the trust that existed outside contracts. Formalise it and you extract the function while destroying the foundation.

But even when the development industry studies these systems correctly, it misses something. It sees the structure and misses the labour. It documents the esusu but not the woman who organises it, who chases the defaulters, who mediates when someone's contribution is late, who holds the group together for twenty years through marriages and deaths and economic crises. That labour is not in the model. The model treats it as a constant, a background condition, something that will simply always be there.

It will not always be there. It is exhaustible. And it is being exhausted.

The young women who watched their mothers give everything to the community are making different calculations. They are moving to cities. They are building careers. They are choosing, rationally, not to inherit the role of unpaid infrastructure.

This is not a betrayal. It is a correction.

But the correction is arriving before any formal system has been built to replace what the informal system provided. The load-bearing wall is being removed. Nobody has put up a column.

The infrastructure crisis coming to African communities is not about roads or electricity, though those matter. It is about the collapse of the unpaid systems that have always compensated for the failure of formal ones. And it is arriving quietly, without a budget line or a policy response, because the system it is replacing was never officially counted in the first place.

In 1995, the United Nations estimated that if unpaid work were included in national accounting, it would add approximately $16 trillion to the global economy. The figure has been updated since. It has always been large. It has never changed the way we measure things.

This is worth sitting with.

Not as a statistics problem. As a choice.

GDP — Gross Domestic Product — is not a neutral instrument. It was designed in the 1930s, by economists working primarily in Western contexts, to measure market transactions. Things that are bought and sold. It was never designed to measure care, or community, or the work of maintaining human life. Its designers knew this. They said so. They noted, explicitly, that the measurement excluded household labour, and they left it excluded — because including it was complicated, and because the people doing it were not the people whose economic activity was considered worth measuring.

That decision — made in the 1930s, in rooms where no African woman sat — is still running. It is the installer that Part Three of the previous series warned you about. It is a default so embedded in the global economic system that questioning it still reads, in mainstream policy circles, as radical.

But it is not radical to ask: if your measurement tool cannot see the majority of the work that keeps societies functioning, is it measuring what you think it is measuring?

Consider what Nigerian GDP counts and what it does not.

It counts the hotel but not the grandmother who raised the children of three generations of that family so that their parents could work. It counts the hospital but not the woman who sat for six months nursing her husband back to health and saved the health system the cost of that care. It counts the commercial farm but not the smallholder woman who feeds her community from a plot the size of a sitting room, using knowledge passed down across generations, producing food that never enters a formal market and therefore does not exist, economically speaking.

It counts the church building but not the pastoral care network, run almost entirely by women, that provides mental health support to communities that cannot afford a therapist. It counts the school but not the lesson notes written by hand at midnight, the uniforms washed and ironed, the school fees borrowed from the esusu, the child walked to school every morning by a mother who never completed her own education.

None of this is in the number. The number gets cited in policy documents, development reports, investment pitches. The number shapes decisions about where resources go and who deserves them. The number is the reality that governments manage toward.

And the number cannot see her.

There is a secondary erasure that happens alongside the economic one.

When a society consistently fails to count certain labour, it also fails to protect the people doing it. The woman whose work is invisible to the economy is also invisible to the law, to social protection systems, to pension frameworks, to healthcare policy designed around formal employment. She has no maternity leave because she has no employer. She has no pension because she had no salary. She has no occupational health protection because her occupation was never recorded.

She gave forty years to her community. The community's GDP grew. Her contribution to that growth is unrecorded. She arrives at old age with nothing the formal system will acknowledge, dependent on the same informal networks she spent her life maintaining — networks that are themselves under pressure, themselves thinning, themselves running low on the young women who used to replenish them.

The erasure compounds. It does not stay in the spreadsheet. It follows her home.

There have been efforts to fix this. Feminist economists have been building alternative measurement frameworks for decades. Time-use surveys. Satellite accounts for household production. Care economy indices. Some governments have piloted inclusion of unpaid work in their national accounts. The tools exist.

They are not being used. Not at scale. Not in ways that change policy. Not in Africa, where the gap between what is measured and what is real is widest and the consequences of that gap are most severe.

Because measurement is not a technical problem. It is a political one. To count the unpaid contributor is to acknowledge the debt. And acknowledging the debt would require paying it. And paying it — in policy, in resources, in structural recognition — would mean redistributing something from the people who currently benefit from the invisibility.

The spreadsheet is not broken. The spreadsheet is working exactly as designed.

Let us say, for a moment, that we decided to actually reckon with this.

Not as a sentiment. Not as a speech at a women's day event, with applause and photographs and nothing that changes on Monday morning. As a structural reckoning. The kind that requires something from the people who have benefited from the invisibility. The kind with a cost.

What would it look like?

It would look like counting. Genuinely, systematically counting the unpaid labour that holds African communities together — through time-use surveys, through care economy accounting, through the kind of measurement infrastructure that makes invisible work visible in the data that governments actually use to make decisions. Not as an academic exercise. As a prerequisite for policy.

You cannot fund what you have not measured. You cannot protect what the system does not see. The first reckoning is epistemic: we have to be willing to know the full picture, even when the full picture is indicting.

It would look like redistribution. Not charity — redistribution. The community that has extracted decades of uncompensated labour from its women owes something. The state that has relied on informal care networks to compensate for its failures in health, education, and social protection owes something. The economy that has grown on the back of unpaid reproductive labour owes something.

What it owes, concretely, is investment. In care infrastructure. In pensions for women whose working lives were spent outside formal employment. In health systems that do not assume an unpaid female carer will fill every gap. In social protection that covers the person who raised five children and cared for two parents and organised the community and never received a salary, because her work was called love and love is not supposed to have a price.

Love does not have a price. But labour does. And the two have been deliberately confused for a very long time.

It would look like changing what we celebrate.

African culture celebrates the builder, the provider, the man who paid the school fees. This is not wrong — those contributions are real. But the celebration is incomplete. It has cropped the image. The full picture includes the woman who made the building habitable, who made the provider functional, who ensured that the child whose fees were paid actually arrived at school ready to learn.

To change what we celebrate is not to diminish anyone. It is to enlarge the frame. To let the full picture develop.

This is cultural work. It is slower than policy. It happens in the stories we tell, the people we name things after, the histories we teach in schools, the values we pass to children about whose labour matters and whose is simply expected. It cannot be legislated. But it can be chosen — by families, by communities, by writers and teachers and parents who decide that the full story is worth telling.

It would look like listening.

The women who carry this labour have, in most cases, never been asked what they need. They have been thanked, occasionally. They have been celebrated, briefly. They have been studied, sometimes. But the policy rooms where decisions are made about African development — about what gets funded, what gets measured, what gets built — those rooms have not been designed with their presence in mind.

Listening would mean changing who is in the room. Not symbolically — not one woman at a table of twenty men who will not adjust the agenda. Substantively. The people whose labour is under discussion should be leading the discussion.

This seems obvious. It remains rare.

None of this will happen quickly. The systems that benefit from unpaid labour are old and deeply embedded. The measurement tools exist but are not deployed. The policy frameworks would require political will that is not currently present. The cultural change is generational.

But the alternative is a specific kind of collapse.

The young women making different calculations — choosing cities, choosing careers, choosing not to inherit the role of unpaid infrastructure — are not going to reverse that choice because someone asks them nicely. The load-bearing wall is coming down. The only question is whether anything gets built to replace it before the roof falls in.

The reckoning is not optional. It is only a question of whether it is chosen or arrived at through crisis.

So this is the commit message.

Not a thank you that asks nothing. A thank you that acknowledges the debt, names the debtor, and begins the work of repayment.

To the woman who woke before everyone else. Who fed the meeting and settled the dispute and sat with the grief and organised the contribution and stretched the food and walked the child and held the thing together when everything else was falling apart.
